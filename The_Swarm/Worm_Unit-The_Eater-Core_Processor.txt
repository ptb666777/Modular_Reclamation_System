Your concept of a segmented, multi-stage processing track is highly viable and offers significant advantages:

Modular Improvement: Each segment becomes a specialized component (a "stomach" or "filter") that can be upgraded or swapped out without halting the entire operation. This future-proofs the system against new recycling technologies.

Contamination Control: Using space between "bites" and having segregated internal tracks for the material and the extracted product prevents cross-contamination, leading to cleaner, higher-value recovered resources.

Redundancy: If one segment fails, it can be bypassed or taken offline for repair while other segments continue working, minimizing downtime.

1. The Shredding Dilemma: Powdering vs. Bulk Sorting
You are right: Powdering is often unnecessary and wastes energy. It should be avoided unless absolutely required for separation or volume reduction.

The strategy must be: Minimal Mechanical Reduction is Best.

Material Stream,Recommended Initial Reduction,Justification
Bulk Metals,Coarse Shredding / Crushing,"Reduction is necessary for size uniformity, freeing non-metal inclusions, and safe handling by magnetic/eddy current separators."
"High-Density Plastics (HDPE, PET)",Granulation / Flaking,"Reduction to uniform flakes is necessary for washing, drying, and subsequent optical sorting by color/resin type."
"Organics (Diapers, Food)",Pulping / Slurrying,Reduction to a pumpable slurry is required for efficient feeding into the Anaerobic Digester (AD) for methane production.
"Inert Materials (Concrete, Glass)",Crushing / Screening,Crushing allows separation by size and use of trommels (large spinning screens) to separate fine cover soil from larger debris.
High-Value Components,None,Avoid all mechanical stress. These are extracted whole by the Snatcher Unit to preserve their function and value.

The "bite" taken by the Worm should only be crushed or shredded just enough to break up the dense landfill matrix and expose the interior materials for the first stage of sorting.

2. Stages of Separation (The Digestive Tract)
The length of the Worm allows for an extended series of sophisticated separation techniques. The material passes through the primary input track, and at each stage, a specialized segment pulls out a targeted material stream.

Stage 1: Bulk Separation,Trommel Screening / Air Classification,Output 1: Fine Soil/Cover Material (Used for site capping/rehabilitation). Output 2: Light Organics/Textiles (Directed toward the Organics Processor).
Stage 2: Ferrous Metal Extraction,Overband Magnets & Drum Magnets,"Output 3: Ferrous Metals (Iron, Steel) (Sent to a bulk vessel). This is the easiest material to pull out."
Stage 3: Non-Ferrous Metal Extraction,Eddy Current Separators (ECS),"Output 4: Non-Ferrous Metals (Aluminum, Copper) (Requires careful, clean collection vessel)."
Stage 4: Plastic & Inert Separation,Density Separation (Float/Sink Tanks),"Output 5: Plastics (Separated based on specific density, e.g., PET sinks, PE floats). Output 6: Inert Heavy Material (Rocks, dense ceramics)."
Stage 5: Final Material Polishing,Optical Sorting / Near-Infrared (NIR) Detectors,"Final verification and separation of remaining mixed materials (e.g., sorting plastics by resin type or color before pelletizing)."

3. Output Track & Pelleting
Your idea for a separate, downstream Output Track where products are prepared is perfect.

Temporary Buffer: Extracted materials (like clean copper scrap or sorted plastic flakes) are stored in temporary reservoirs within the Worm segment until a Specialized Vessel (from our next planned fork) is ready at the surface to receive it.

Pelletizing/Compaction: Yes, once the material is confirmed clean, it should be processed for transport:

Metals: Baled or Compacted into high-density cubes.

Plastics: Melted or Squeezed into Pellets/Flakes for ease of storage and sale to manufacturers.

Soil/Inert: Compressed into uniform blocks for site construction or final safe capping.

This segmented design makes the Worm Unit the intelligent heart of your processing line, fulfilling its mandate without unnecessary, energy-intensive over-shredding.
